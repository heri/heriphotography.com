Panasonic GH3 field test – The perfect go-to camera

January 20, 2014 Photography, Video Gear 1080p Olympus 45mm f1.8 Panasonic Panasonic 20mm f1.7 pancake Panasonic GH3 2 Comments

Under rain, snow or ice, the GH3 camera will give you great videos and photos

I’ve had the Panasonic GH3 for 9 months. I have used it in a major photography project, music shows, as well as music and corporate videos. To put it simply, it’s a workhorse that will produce reliable results, in all possible conditions.

Of course, for every feature mentioned here, you can always find cameras with better specifications. It not as weather-proof as the GoPro, doesn’t shoot super slow motion video like the Sony FS700, and it can’t be confused with a 1DX or a D800E for glossy editorial work. However, I can challenge you to find a camera that has the most balanced set of specifications. The Panasonic will impress you in 99% of photography situations.

My History with Panasonic Cameras

Back when I had the full frame Canon 5D Mark II, I was longing for a lighter camera that could be taken anywhere. The new Panasonic GF1 with its small size and micro 43 sensor was designed for that mission. With the 20mm pancake lens, it’s the camera you always have in your bag or in the pocket of your winter coat. The results were good enough for any kind of work. Of course, the Canon 5D Mark II always produced creamier pictures but there’s a great feeling of satisfaction when you can carry a camera anywhere and know that the pictures will be good enough.

I loved and respected Panasonic for giving me that pleasure.

A while later, I discovered videography and film-making, a whole new set of challenges. The Panasonic GH2 delivered great quality in that regard, in a size that was very similar to the GF1. A large community of users contributed in enhancing the camera and using it for feature films.

The GH2 had a blue-ish cast though, and its 2.5mm audio in drove me mad. The Panasonic GH3 solved those problems, upgraded the body to pro level, brought in new efficient codecs. It’s not pocketable anymore, but I have now the Canon EOS-M for that need.

Winter at -40C

The 2013 winter brought in -25C to -40C temperatures, with the wind chill. To make most of it, we planned a day of snowshoes as well as sledding. That’s how Canadian winters should be enjoyed! So off I went in a winter coat and show pants, with a Panasonic GH3 + 20mm pancake lens around my neck.

The 20mm is wide enough that even by hand-holding the camera, you can manage to get usable footage that are not too shaky.

The Panasonic GH3 lets you enjoy the moment; instead of being self-centered in the act of photography.

Video shot at 1080p 24p with 20mm pancake, in Aperture Priority, Auto ISO. Make sure to switch to 1080p when watching it.

It was snowing that day, plus there was also copious amounts of ice and snow that went into the camera while sledding. It bumped into rocks, and my fingers were numb from coldbite, yet it performed flawelessly. The camera was always ready on my neck.

How I use the GH3

I use the following for the GH3:

  • Panasonic Lumix 20mm f1.7 lens. It’s a normal/wide angle lens equivalent to 40mm in full-frame. The F1.7 aperture works great for any situation – with the lens sharp enough for any situation
  • Olympus 45mm f1.8 is a perfect portrait lens that compliments well the Panasonic. Optically, the lens is transparent (read extended review) and focuses fast. The only caveat is the lack of Image stabilization
  • The Panasonic Battery Grip is essential for portrait shooting and also for extended work. It’s pricy though so for most users, just buying a second battery makes sense.
  • the Rode VideoMic Pro is a shotgun that plugs directly into the 3.5mm audio in. It’s great for quick interviews and for events
  • Sony 32GB Class 10 SD Cards. Fast cards are necessary if you want to use the GH3′s hungry codecs
  • The Olympus FL-600R flash can be used wirelessly (great for portraits, bounce and many other techniques) and also has a handy LED light for video

All in all, it’s a small kit that’s much cheaper and lighter than a Nikon D800 with a normal lens.

I always use the Panasonic on Aperture Priority and Auto-ISO, with maximum ISO set at 3200. The main reason I am not using full manual is speed, since I don’t want to loose a moment because I’m fiddling with exposure. When set on Aperture priority, all you have to do is focus and get the moment.

I mostly shot between f1.7 and f2.8 on the Panasonic lens, depending on the shot. A low-light or portrait is going to be at f1.7 while group shots, landscapes are at f2.8. I dial with the thumb to change the aperture.

The grip of the GH3 makes handholding easier, with little fatigue and no micro jitters.

For videos, I switch to the dedicated movie mode, at 50mpbs. After a few tests, 50mbps is actually cleaner and better than the 72mbps. Here’s the process for videos:

  1. I mostly shot with the rotating screen, using the Gh3 like a mini C100. The left hand goes around the touch screen to select the point of focus
  2. I don’t use a rig so I make sure my arms are locked around my body
  3. I dial down the aperture I want, depending on the shot
  4. If I record sound, I check that levels are ok. You can also use headphones with the GH3 to check the sound, but nothing beats seeing the levels on screen
  5. I focus by half-pressing the shutter button. I don’t manually focus or zoom in the screen, as I believe that’s only when you have a big 4″ or 7″ monitor and when working in narrative or commercial.
  6. There’s a half-second lag when starting or stopping recording on the GH3 – so I shoot well before the desired moment and stop recording seconds after
  7. The GH3 can accomodate well aperture changes – if you notice that you need to put in more light or if you think that the depth of field is too shallow to cover your subject

Timeless portrait photography project

I did a major photo project with the Panasonic GH3 — photos were taken to have a painturesque element to them.

I have the 85mm f1.2L – THE lens that most amateur and pro photographers associate to the ultimate portrait lens.

I find however that the Olympus 45mm f1.8 is better than the Canon 85mm. The bokeh of the 85mm is too noticeable and takes away the focus from the subject. The Olympus also has more micro-contrast that makes details pop.

The small size of the Gh3 + 45mm Olympus lens lets you connect directly to people on a human level.

What I love about the Gh3

COLORS – The Panasonic GH2 had overall a blue/green-ish tint, especially indoors, so subjects always looked sick. The images from GH3 don’t have that problem and they even possess a bit of that full-frame look.

STRONG IMAGE – The images are usable without transcoding and publishable right from the memory card, but they can also be corrected and many creative effects can be added, without the image breaking up. Simply, you don’t have to worry that the images will not be usable in post-production.

RESOLUTION – Compared to other DSLRs (Canon, Nikon, Sony etc.), the GH3 has much more resolution. It’s like going from 720p to real 1080p!

BUILD – The camera is water-proof and dust-proof. It’s also bigger and overall feels less plasticky than the GH2. The general public always complement on the pro look of the camera.

GOOD LENSES CHOICES – I use mainly the Panasonic 20mm pancake. It’s small, bright and sharp. For portraits, I use the Olympus 45mm f1.8. But the lenses choice is not limited to Panasonic or Olympus. There’s also Voigtlander, SLR Magic, Sigma as well as Canon/Nikon if you are ok with using adapters. Because it’s a mirrorless mount, any lens will fit on the GH3

TELE – When shooting video, you can switch quickly to the 1:1 crop mode, which is equivalent to a 3x digital zoom. You don’t loose in resolution, however it could be a stop noisier. On the Gh3, a wide-angle lens can be switched to a telephoto/portrait lens within seconds. A great feature for event coverage where you need to take shots of the environment and groups, and sometimes also details and portraits of attendees.

1080p 60fps – The Gh3 is one of the rare video cameras that can be used for slow-motion 1080p videos. Meanwhile, Canon or Nikon cameras need to be switched to 720p

WEATHER PROOF – The Panasonic Gh3 is water and dust-proof. It also works very well in sub-zero temperature. Great for sports and outdoors!

What I don’t love about the Gh3

NO OPTICAL IMAGE STABILIZATION – I lose 25% of shots because it’s not stable enough. Meanwhile, Olympus and Sony have integrated IBIS and other latest technologies in their bodies. Serious caveat!!

MOIRÉ – Despite all of its strengths, its predecessor GH2 has less moiré. What gives Panasonic?

SMALL MONITOR / NO WAVEFORM – the monitor is very small which means you will probably end up buying an external monitor or EVF. Also it doesn’t have handy features as waveform or vectroscope.

NO XLR – there’s no standard way of recording dual-channel XLR audio to the GH3.

NO ND FIlTER – Why do we have to buy graduated ND Filter for each lens we have? It would be much easier to integrate a ND filter in the body.

4:2:0 8 BITS HDMI OUT – the HDMI out is useless for external recording as the quality won’t be much better than the internal codec. Not a big deal, but having 4:2:2 10 bits would make the camera usable for speciality shots.

NO FOCUS PEAKING – I trust the autofocus for photos and videos but when moving the camera or following a subject, focus peaking would make the camera much more usable

A Workhorse with excellent results

In summary, the GH3 :

  • is tough enough to withstand any weather
  • can handle any situation (portrait, landscape, events) with the 20mm pancake lens
  • produces beautiful RAW pictures
  • has the best video codec in the market, amongst DSLRs
  • doesn’t intimidate subjects, unlike other pro cameras
  • is a lot of fun to use

As you can guess, I love working with this camera and it’s my go-to camera when I want to get reliable results.

Rating : 4.5/5 Highly recommended as a main camera
