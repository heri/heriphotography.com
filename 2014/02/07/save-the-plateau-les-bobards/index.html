Save The Plateau : Les Bobards

February 7, 2014 Photography montreal Photo report 0 Comments

Les Bobards has been for the past 20 years a cultural and artistic landmark of Montreal. For newcomers to Montreal, as well as established Montrealers, going to Les Bobards is always something special – where you discover Montreal’s uniqueness, a blend of fiercely independent and world music, a celebration of diversity, right in the heart of the Plateau. There’s always something special where a stage is just nearby a restaurant, an art gallery, or a few blocks from your apartment.

Artists like the celebrated group Nomadic Massive made regular appearances at Les Bobards, making it part of its style:

In the past few months though, there has been a battle between new residents and venues like Les Bobards, with the idea that Le Plateau should become a “calm” & safe residential neighborhood, i.e. a rampant gentrification of what is one of Montreal’s most vibrant neighborhood.

This conflict reached its peak a few days ago with Les Bobards receiving a letter from the city forbidding them from (1) charging a cover charge (2) having a dance floor (3) advertising a lineup of shows. This essentially means the closing of the venue and a loss of the cultural landmark.

Les Bobards welcomed many artists to celebrate their new album releases. Here local artists, cultural workers & the whole community met for Beyond Monke-e’s album release:

Les Bobards is also a venue where teenagers and aspiring youth were welcomed to perform, for them often the first time they were performing publicly. Here we’ve got a hip-hop group from la Maison des Jeunes de Cotes-des-Neiges:

Les Bobards also participated to Montreal’s global cultural image, by welcoming international artists like Brazilian Rael de Rima. Very few places are as accommodating as Les Bobards, with the mix of convenience, affordability, and good infrastructure for musicians:

Les Bobards hosted monthly or weekly local bands, such as the group Bambara Trans, supporting them in their art. Having regular access to an accessible venue is crucial for young artists, when access to grants is more and more limited:

Here, Montreal activist and artist Emrical (with Vincent Stephen-Ong) performs Mon Rêve :

Les Bobards also hosts Kalmunity and similar shows like the Hip-Hop revival series, with dozens of rappers, artists, beatmakers, musicians getting together – contributing to build Montreal’s cultural fabric:

Les Bobards is often packed, one of the main reasons being the venue’s accessibility, right at the center of the Plateau. Many young artists and their fans don’t have cars, which makes Les Bobards their favorite venue:

Les Bobards is not always *loud* music – it also hosts slam sessions, poetry and cultural events. Here Jazz saxophonist and poet Jason “Blackbird” Selman:

It takes only one visit to see that Les Bobards makes Montreal what it is, a mirror of its diversity and inclusiveness. Rarely we see such a diverse palette of musical styles and artists:
